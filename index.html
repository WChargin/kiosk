Model UN redesigned

While Model United Nations conferences excel on a variety of levels – education, awareness, inspiration – one area where conferences commonly fall short is use of technology. Chairs attempt to use a variety of independent tools, like online stopwatches, PowerPoint, and Windows Notepad to manage the debate, but this often results in slow, error-prone workflows that either leave delegates uninformed or subject them to information overload.

Kiosk aims to change that.

Kiosk is an application suite that integrates everything a chair could need into a single application for the main chair, and companion applications for assistant chairs, rapporteurs, and other dais members.

Kiosk is written in Java, and as such is available for Windows, Mac, and Linux operating systems (and pretty much anything else too, really).

Feature overview

Some of Kiosk's main features include:

  • Automatic motion management. Motion order is a critical yet often overlooked facet of parliamentary procedure, and even experienced chairs sometimes fail to recall the correct order in which motions should be entertained. Kiosk automatically orders, votes on, and executes any motions on the floor. Kiosk also knows which type of majority (half-plus-one or qualified/2&frasl;3) each motion requires.

  • Crisis management. Chairs of stagnant or one-sided debates often manufacture and introduce "crises" to spur the committee onward. Kiosk integrates the process of creating, assigning, and deploying crises, complete with opportunities for projected fake (or real) news articles, guest speakers, and Q&A sessions.

  • Working papers and resolutions. Kiosk allows chairs to entertain motions to introduce working papers submitted as PDF files or published with Google Docs. Delegates can then hold formal caucuses to discuss the papers, in which the relevant papers will automatically be projected. If delegates use Google Docs, the displayed paper will be automatically updated.

  • Speech analysis. If a committee has multiple chairs, one can be designated as the speech analyzer. By opening the Speech Analyzer companion application, the chair will see a record of each speech as it is begun, and can analyze it on various qualities in real-time. The speech records can then be exported to CSV format (human-readable and supported by Excel, Google Sheets, and LibreOffice Calc), and the chairs can see real-time graphs of how often each delegate has spoken.

  • Transparency. To mitigate information overload or sparsity, Kiosk provides a separate Public Panel that can be projected for the delegates to see, while Kiosk's controls remain visible to the chairs only. The public panel adjusts its view according to the current context, and always displays exactly what delegates need to know at any given time – no more, no less – in a clear and elegant format.

Check out the Kiosk user manual for more features, or look below for an analysis of features mentioned above.

Feature analysis

Automatic motion management

Motions should be entertained in order from most disruptive to least disruptive... but what does that mean? Clearly, an unmoderated caucus is more disruptive than a moderated caucus, but where does a Motion to Divide the Question fall in there? How about a Motion to Change the Speaking Time?

Kiosk's got you covered. The PrecedenceComparator class has a built-in ordering of motions. Whenever you add a motion to the floor, Kiosk will update the order of the list of motions on the floor to make sure the motions are sorted. Then, when you've added all the motions you'd like to entertain, simply press the "Process Motions" button and you'll vote on each motion, in order, one at a time, until one is passed. In the voting dialog, Kiosk also lets you know whether a motion is procedural or substantive (indicative of whether or not a delegate may abstain) and whether the motion requires a simple majority (more than half of the voting delegates must vote in favor) or a qualified majority (two-thirds or more of the voting delegates must vote in favor).

If the motion performs an action such as moving into a caucus, changing the topic, or changing the speaking time, Kiosk will update the committee for you. Moderated caucuses have their own interface wherein the chairs can select speakers from a list of all present delegates, and two timers: one for the caucus overall, and one for the individual speakers. Unmoderated caucuses simply display a time bar.

Motions will also be projected on the Public Panel for delegates to see.

Crisis management

Need to introduce a crisis to your committee? Simply switch the view from "Standard Debate" to "View Crises" and you can design your crises without the delegates seeing. You can have as many crises stored as you want, and to each one you can attach a briefing to be displayed when the crises is deployed, as well as configure the guest speaker (if applicable) and Q&A session settings.

Whenever you're ready, just hit the "Deploy" button and the Public Panel's contents will immediately be replaced by a Crisis view. This will display the briefing, if any, and contain information on the guest speaker and Q&A session timing (if any). When the crisis is over, the panel will return to the standard view.
